ü§î Why does this happen?

Computers store numbers in binary (base 2), but we write numbers in decimal (base 10).

Just like 1/3 = 0.333... goes on forever in decimal, the number 0.1 goes on forever in binary:

0.1 in binary = 0.0001100110011001100110011... (repeating forever!)

Since computers have limited memory, they have to round these infinite sequences. When you add two rounded numbers together, those tiny errors can accumulate and show up in the result.

üí° Think of it like this: If you round 0.333 + 0.333 + 0.334 you get 1.000, but that's not exactly 1/3 + 1/3 + 1/3 = 1. Same idea!

Why the error?

0.1 and 0.2 can't be represented exactly in binary!

It's like trying to write 1/3 in decimal: 0.3333...

How it works:

Floats use scientific notation in binary.

They store: sign, exponent, and fraction

‚ÑπÔ∏è Floats can have decimals but may have tiny precision errors.
